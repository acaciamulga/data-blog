GBIF Regional Statistics - 2020

John Waller
2020-05-11
GBIF

Iceland Index. A plot of raw species counts from equal-area hexagons approximately the size of Iceland. This plot excludes international waters, but includes countries EEZ areas. We see here that while Iceland has >5K species other areas within Amazonia, Eastern Europe, and Africa have suspiciously fewer, indicating a likely data gap.

What are the GBIF regions?

jpg | pdf | svg | code

The GBIF Network is divided into regions. These regions are derived from the IPBES regional assessments, shown on this map. This post will cover countries/areas with some political status and not on other GBIF Affiliates and Antarctica.

Occurrence and Species counts

jpg | pdf | svg | code

Europe & Central Asia and North America have by far the most occurrences about/published in their regions. A large part of these records are from bird observations, which is reflected in the species-count graph below.

jpg | pdf | svg | code

Latin America & The Caribbean have the most species about their region, which makes sense from a species-richness perspective. Europe & Central Asia and North America publish the most species, which could occur in any region or in international waters.

Birds dominate occurrence totals

jpg | pdf | svg | code

Birds, of course, dominate occurrence records in almost all countries and regions, particularly in North America. I plot the same graph below excluding birds and countries with less than 1 million total occurrences.

jpg | pdf | svg | code

A large red bar indicates that the country has many occurrences about the country that are not in the top most popular groups (Birds, Plants, Insects, Mammals, Molluscs, Fungi). Other records are usually fish, amphibians, or reptiles. These graphs largely show a publishing bias towards the familar, which is not surprising. One can search for a country (e.g. The United States) on the GBIF web interface and get a more detailed view of that country’s taxanomic breakdown.

Citations generated from publishing

jpg | pdf | svg | code

Occurrences published from Latin America & The Caribbean, Oceania, Asia, and Africa generate a disproportionate number of citations.

Species gains over time

jpg | pdf | svg | code

GBIF regions gain around 25-50K new species every two years. In 2022-2024 Africa will have the same number of species as North America had on GBIF in 2016-2018.
